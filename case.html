Live Activity: Real-Time Order Tracking

If you don't have time to go through the entire case, click on the button below to see the results straight away

Challenge

Problem

Users often lose interest in their order after placing it – they don't know when it will be ready, packed, or shipped. This lack of transparency leads to support calls, negative reviews, and reduced customer loyalty.

Objective

Implement Live Activities that keep users informed about their order status without requiring them to open the app – even when the device is locked or the app is running in the background.

Design process

[ 01 ] UX research

Live Activity is a native iOS feature provided by Apple, and at the time of this feature's development, there is no official Android equivalent.

Therefore, during the research phase, I identified two main objectives:

  • Thoroughly examine all the nuances of how Live Activity is displayed and how it works on IOS, so that during the design process I could propose the most relevant and effective solutions, as well as account for all its specifics
  • Investigate how this functionality could be implemented for Android.

Summary

The knowledge gained during the research stage became the foundation for all subsequent stages.

[ 02 ] User Scenarios & States Mapping

When making a purchase, the order goes through specific statuses within the app, and all of these statuses have been implemented in Live Activities:

A unique visual element was designed for each status:

1. Icon (see Chapter 4)

2. Heading

3. Text message (e.g. "Your order is on the way")

4. Progress bar (helps to determine the order's status in a few seconds)

[ 03 ] Platform-Specific Design

IOS:

1. Live Activities are available starting from iOS 16.1;

2. Live Activities support four display modes: one on the Lock Screen and three in the Dynamic Island area (on iPhone 14 Pro and newer models).

Display modes within the Dynamic Island:

  • Compact – space is allocated both to the left and right of the island.
  • Minimal – space is allocated either only to the left or only to the right of the island. This area is quite limited and typically used for an icon, a compact representation of an interactive action, or a short text snippet.
  • Expanded – an expanded view that appears upon a long press or tap, or triggered by an external event.

It was crucial to carefully design each display mode.

The Expanded mode retains all elements, similar to the Live Activity on the Lock Screen, with minor adjustments aligned with Apple's Human Interface Guidelines (HIG):

  • Increased corner radius
  • Black background color
  • A more compact logo

Compact and Minimal presentation

For these display modes, the goal was to ensure app recognizability and clearly indicate order progress within a very limited space.

The key element is a circular progress bar – an intuitive metaphor that effectively symbolizes an ongoing process.

In the Minimal presentation, the app logo is placed inside the progress bar, creating a distinctive brand identifier that clearly informs the user which app the notification came from.

[ 04 ] AI Assets

Images in Live Activities play a crucial role – they help users understand the interface even without reading text.

The following criteria were established for the icons:

  • Instant recognizability: Users should immediately understand their order's current stage, even with a quick glance at the notification;
  • Consistent visual style across all icons;
  • Alignment with the overall app design language;
  • And most importantly, this process shouldn't take too much time.

Conclusion

Therefore, the best solution in this case is to use AI.

[ 05 ] Design System

Component Architecture Considerations

To avoid creating one monolithic "Frankenstein" component, I applied a system approach and instead of a single component, I created four smaller:

1. IOS Live Activity;

2. Android Live Activity;

3. Dynamic Island Minimal (encompassing three states: Compact, Minimal Attached, and Minimal Detached);

4. Dynamic Island Component (expanded view)

What does this give us?

This approach makes our system more flexible and easily scalable.

Live Activities are also an example of a feature that requires platform-specific component variations. While each version conveys the same core information, it operates within its own platform-specific environment and constraints.

The primary properties driving these components are:

  • Delivery type (Planned, Express, Pickup);
  • Order status.

It's also worth noting that each component group is built upon its own base component, which encapsulates shared logic, data structure, and core behavior – all other variants inherit from this base.

In turn, this base component is composed of even smaller, atomic elements (atoms).

Additionally, an animated prototype was developed to demonstrate transitions between states (progress changes, status updates, image swaps), and a detailed technical specification was prepared for developers.
